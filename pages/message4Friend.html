“We hairless apes coevolved with our cloth. From the moment we're wrapped in a blanket at birth, we are surrounded by textiles. They cover our bodies, bedeck our beds, and carpet our floors. Textiles give us seat belts and sofa cushions, tents and bath towels, medical masks and duct tape. They are everywhere. "But, to reverse Arthur C. Clarkes famous adage about magic, any sufficiently familiar technology is indistinguishable from nature. It seems intuitive, obvious so woven into the fabric of our lives that we take it for granted. We no more imagine a world without cloth than one without sunlight or rain.” This first statement in the preface is important to understand because in the preface and prologues of all books, the underlying framework and biases of the author are clearly laid out. The very first sentence “we hairless apes coevolved with our cloth” shows the author’s underlying assumption that evolution is true. The second paragraph hearkens to the very first quote in the preface of the book saying that any “sufficiently familiar technology is indistinguishable from nature.” This is collapsing Arthur C. Clarke’s famous quote from being one about “magic” to one about “nature”, implying that evolution and nature are what drive the development of technologies forward. It should be noted that there is no justification put forth for this assertion. In other words, the author is writing for a particular audience that has already bought into this idea and doesn’t feel the need to defend it at all. The author is probably thinking, “Everyone knows evolution is true and it is obvious so I will assert that evolution is responsible for the development of textile technology and everyone will accept it.” I’m not going to drag this out too much but I will provide the logical framework that refutes this underlying assumption so that we can at least understand that any reference to evolution being the driver of this development of technology is erroneous on the face of it, because this will be referenced later in the book in order to gloss over and avoid more interesting discussions. Darwin was heavily influenced by ancient Hinduism, specifically the idea that “given enough time and space, anything can happen, no matter how improbable”. I agree with this logical statement. And the idea of evolution over infinite time and space becomes a possibility with this underlying assumption. The only problem is that scientific evidence refutes the idea that we live in a universe of “infinite time and space”. Rather, the scientific evidence of astronomical red shifts and primordial universal radio frequencies shows that the universe originated from a single point in time and space about 13 billion years ago in big bang and expanded forward from that moment in time. Therefore, in order to reach the level of evolution that we arrive at today, we only have 13 billion years to work with. But there’s even less time than that for life to even begin to exist at all. Because for the first 1 to 2 billion years, stars and planets would be forming still. So we have about 11 to 12 billion years during which life could have come into existence. But how did life start exactly? Well, let’s look at the simplest organic element, the cell which is fairly complex in and of itself, consisting of many organelles such as mitochondria working together in a harmonious way. The cell is essentially built through protein synthesis, amino acids coming together in a particular sequence and being folded in a particular way, almost like they were programmed to do that (which begs the question, Is there a programmer at work here?). The odds that this would occur spontaneously within even a 13 billion year time period is essentially impossible; a length 100 amino-acid sequence for a protein that must be folded in just the right way when there are 20 ^ 100 possible sequences is impossible. Modern scientists are still working on how this could be possible by asserting that sequences do not necessarily need to align uniquely in combinatorial mathematical space, rather small sequences could already exist somehow within a small area like a pond, and that these combination of sequences is more plausible. Still, the mathematics for even this to occur is vanishingly small. I will even allow for a greater than 4.5 billion year timeframe for this to occur (the current time that it is understood for the earth to come into existence from today), because there are ideas like panspermia which asset that life could have occurred from another planet or galaxy. And yet, the mathematics show how improbable it is that we would have the human mind as we have it today. Let’s assume that it happened, the first cell was created spontaneously and then survived miraculously and replicated into a eukaryotic lifeform. Then we would have to come up with an explanation for how unique species of animals come into being and evolve over time rapidly enough for the creation of the human mind to be as we find it today. There are a lot of assumptions that go into this calculation as well. Natural selection and the accumulation of beneficial traits through natural selection is the primary argument for such evolution to occur. Personally, I would agree with “microevolution over time”, but I could simply call this “adaptation”. However, I would reject that one species could completely change into another species given enough time and natural selection. Why? Because there is no evidence for this: it has never been observed scientifically or otherwise that a species would change into a completely different species, and there are other scientific evidences such as the Cambrian explosion which directly conflict with the assertion that such species change would occur through natural selection alone. What seems more plausible to me personally is that an intelligent designer or programmer was at work creating each species as we see them today, and that adaptation over time accounts for the changes that we see from the fossil record. Again, it’s important to keep this in mind from the preface of this book, because this explanation is often used as a throwaway argument to gloss over any other questions. It effectively stops people from thinking and asks you to accept something on faith alone instead of the scientific evidences.
